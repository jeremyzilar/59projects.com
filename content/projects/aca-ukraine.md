## Intro

Built systems for the US, EU, and UK State Departments, as part of the Atrocity Crimes Advisory Group (ACA), that helped organizations coordinate their work on war crimes with Ukraine's Prosecutor General.

## The Problem

Three governments backed the effort. Five organizations, more than 150 experts in total, were doing the real work, collecting evidence, building case law expertise, running field investigations, and advising on cases ranging from genocide to cybercrimes to attacks on critical infrastructure. But there was no shared way to see what anyone else was doing.

Large all-hands meetings happened roughly every two months, and in between, people had no reliable way to know who else was in Kyiv at a given time, from which organization, for their own security. Work risked being duplicated. Recommendations to the Prosecutor General's office funneled through only a handful of people, without a clear, shared process behind them. And the volume of case information, especially evidence surfacing on social media, was unlike anything most of these organizations had dealt with before, the eventual caseload would run past 135,000.

Many of the people doing this work had been practicing lawyers in their own countries before the war. Almost overnight, they needed to become experts in war crimes law. Several had grown up in Bosnia and other countries that lived through war in the 1990s, and were now advising Ukraine largely from a distance, aware of how much that distance limited what they could actually do.

## How We Worked

We ran a six-week discovery process, interviewing people across ACA's funding governments, its implementing organizations, and the broader network of people and teams supporting Ukraine's prosecutors. The goal was to find out what people actually needed in order to coordinate, not what a generic internal portal is supposed to have.

What came back again and again was about how any group of independent organizations, working urgently on the same problem, actually stay in sync, who's where, who's doing what, how information gets routed to the people who can act on it, how everyone stays reachable across organizational lines.

Every tool decision went through that same lens. Scheduling across time zones for the secretariat team led to Calendly. Keeping a wide range of meetings secure and easy to join led to Zoom, paired with Otter.ai so meetings got recorded, transcribed, and turned into searchable notes automatically. Housing shared information across five separate organizations, each with its own comfort level and security posture, led to Notion for the working handbook, synced together so nothing lived in two places at once. 

None of these were obvious defaults. Each came with real tradeoffs we wrote down and weighed before choosing, and permissions across organizations needed real guardrails.

## What Shipped

We designed and built a public website for ACA and an internal handbook that became the coalition's shared operating system. It was organized around ACA's priority crime categories, genocide, conflict-related sexual violence, cybercrimes, attacks on critical infrastructure, and more, and every category got the same structure underneath it, reports and case reviews, key contacts, a shared calendar of events, and a running list of tasks and deadlines. 

Wrapped around that were an operations section (_requesting access, security training, travel, deployments_) and a communications section (_media tracking, talking points, a shared social presence_), plus a weekly update so the whole coalition could see what had actually happened that week.

We tried to write the guidance, protocols, and tools inside it so they weren't locked to this one situation. Whether that instinct proves out elsewhere is still open. What's certain is that the pattern itself, organize by category, give every category the same operating structure, wrap it in shared operations and comms, held together across five organizations and more than 150 people without collapsing back into separate silos.

## What We Learned

The hardest coordination problems rarely come from bad will. The organizations involved wanted to work together. What they lacked was shared infrastructure, a way to see each other's work, a way to route decisions, a way to know who was where. Once that existed, the will was already there to use it.

We also came away with more respect for the people actually doing this work, lawyers who became war crimes experts out of necessity, often carrying their own countries' histories into how they approached it. That's not something a coordination system can fix, but it shaped how carefully the system needed to handle information, and how much it needed to stay out of the way.
